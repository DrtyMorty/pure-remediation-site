The first 24 hours after water damage: what to do, in order

Pure Remediation · 4 min read

Water damage gets worse by the hour, and so does the insurance claim if you handle the first day wrong. Here's the order that protects both your home and your payout.

1. Safety first — before you touch anything

  • If water is near outlets, appliances, or the breaker panel, stay out of the room until the power to that area is off.
  • If the water came from a drain, toilet, or outside flooding, treat it as contaminated — don't wade in without protection.
  • Ceilings sagging with water can drop suddenly. Don't stand under them.

2. Stop the source

Shut off the water at the fixture valve or at the main. If you don't know where your main shutoff is, find it now — in most Houston homes it's at the meter box near the street or where the line enters the house. A burst pipe can put hundreds of gallons into your house per hour.

3. Document before you clean up — this is the one people skip

Your insurance claim is only as strong as your evidence. Before you move a single towel:

  • Photograph and video everything — standing water, the source, damaged contents, waterlines on the walls.
  • Get close-ups of ruined items and wide shots of each room.
  • Keep damaged materials (carpet, drywall cutouts) until your adjuster has seen them or told you in writing you can toss them.

Why it matters: most policies require you to prevent further damage, but they also require proof of the original loss. Photos taken before cleanup do both jobs at once.

4. Call your insurance company

Report the loss the same day. Ask two questions: what your policy covers for emergency mitigation, and whether they need anything specific documented. You have the right to choose your own restoration contractor — insurers may suggest one, but the choice is yours.

5. Get the water out and the air moving

  • Mop, wet-vac, or squeegee standing water if it's clean water and safe to do so.
  • Move furniture off wet carpet; put foil or blocks under the legs of what you can't move.
  • Run the AC and fans — unless the water is contaminated, in which case moving air spreads the problem.

6. Know what a fan can't fix

Household fans dry surfaces. They don't dry the wet drywall, insulation, and framing behind them — and that's where mold starts, usually within 24 to 48 hours. If water got into walls, under floors, or sat for more than a few hours, the structure needs professional drying equipment and moisture readings to verify it's actually dry.
