Does homeowner's insurance cover water damage in Texas?

Pure Remediation · 5 min read

The honest answer: it depends on how the water got in. Texas policies vary, but almost all of them draw the same three lines. Know where your loss falls before you file.

Covered: sudden and accidental

Most homeowner policies cover water damage that happens suddenly and by accident:

  • Burst or frozen pipes
  • A failed water heater, washing machine hose, or AC drain pan
  • Rain entering through storm damage (a hole the wind just made in your roof)
  • An overflowing appliance or fixture

Coverage usually includes the damage the water caused — floors, drywall, contents — and often the cost of tearing out material to reach the broken pipe. It usually does not include fixing the pipe itself.

Usually not covered: gradual damage

The slow leak under the sink that dripped for months, the roof that's been seeping through worn shingles, long-term seepage around a shower pan — insurers treat these as maintenance, not accidents. This is why timing and documentation matter: the earlier a loss is found and reported, the cleaner the claim.

Never covered by homeowner's insurance: flood

Water that rises from outside — bayou overflow, street flooding, storm surge — is excluded from standard homeowner policies everywhere in the U.S., including Texas. Flood coverage is a separate policy, usually through the National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP) or a private flood insurer. In the Houston area, it's worth having even outside mapped flood zones; a large share of flood claims come from properties not officially designated high-risk.

One more Texas wrinkle: the Texas Department of Insurance (tdi.texas.gov) publishes free consumer guides on storm and water claims — a good neutral reference if you disagree with your carrier.

What makes a claim go smoothly

  1. Report fast. Same-day reporting removes the "was this gradual?" question.
  2. Photograph before cleanup. Standing water, the source, waterlines, damaged contents.
  3. Mitigate. Policies require you to prevent further damage — stop the source, extract the water, start drying. Keep receipts; mitigation is typically reimbursable.
  4. Get moisture readings in writing. Adjusters work from documentation, not descriptions. Moisture logs and a line-item scope are what move a claim.
  5. Don't sign over your claim casually. Read anything a contractor asks you to sign, and know that you choose your own restoration company — not your insurer.

The bottom line

Burst pipe? Very likely covered. Slow leak? Probably not. Rising water? Only with separate flood insurance. And in every case, the quality of your documentation decides how much of the covered loss you actually recover.
